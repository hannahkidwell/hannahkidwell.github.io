City and County of Honolulu

We Are Hawai‘i

CX, Interaction Design, Accessibility

This case study is from the early stages of the 2020 Covid Pandemic, in which Hawaii was undergoing high rates of Covid-19 infection. Hawaii is the most diverse state in the United States of America where those speaking English "less than very well" account for 12.4% of the population (which is much higher than the U.S. average of 8.6 percent.), so there was a state-wide lack of informational distribution and awareness.

Three months after the start of the campaign, which included video, way-finding messages, digital advertising, in-game ads, and social media, 9 in 10 people in Oahu had seen the ads. By February 2021, cases were down by ~70%

 

Brief

The City of Honolulu selected one agency to create the entire campaign and propagate the various executions. There was already a website, so the ask was to update the site with a landing page and create an effective online execution.

Overview of the Market: During the early stages of the first wave of the virus in late 2020, many families we still gathering in large groups, and due to the cost and culture- multigenerational housing was contributing to spread within families.

 

Budget & Research

Budget: $120,000.00 Awarded by Hawaii Tourism Authority

Challenges:

Because there is only so much information to be absorbed at a time, we narrowed down the keypoints to Covered, Outside, and Handwashing as the primary points to be absorbed immediately by the residents.

Strategy:

  • General Strategy: derived from the research

  • Stage 1: Focus on Masks, Distancing and Hand-washing

  • Stage 2: Reminder of the Beauty and What Was Being Achieved

  • Stage 3: Gratitude for All that Has Been Done

Recommendations for Accessibility

Title II under the ADA applies to State and local government websites ensuring that the site has accessible features for people with a variety of disabilities. One of the most widely accepted set of website accessibility standards is Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 Level AA Success Criteria. These best practice guidelines are internationally recognized and used as a best practice worldwide.

  • Alert announcement bar color contrast is insufficient. Current contrast ratio is 3.82 out of a required 4.5. The intent is to provide enough color contrast between text and its background so it can be read by people with moderately low vision or have some form of color blindness. These types of users may not necessarily be blind or use assistive technologies like screen readers.

  • Recommendation: instead of using #F406B0 as the background color, use #DE05A0 to meet the color contrast ratio.

  • Avoid using images of text for key content. Content banners currently contain key content that could be deemed essential for users. This type of content should not be presented using images of text but rather as live HTML text. This not only makes the content more consumable by assistive technologies like screen readers, but also makes the content more legible on mobile devices.

  • Recommendation: Recreate content banners for “City & County of Honolulu Information Hotline” and “COVID-19 Email Update” as HTML content.

  • Avoid “breaking” the back button. Many of the current page links in OneOahu.org inconsistently open in new tabs/windows rather than in the same tab/window. This renders the browser “back” button unusable. Usability research studies suggest that “back button failure is one of the commonest causes of abandonment” and “the accessibility issue is that some users get confused with new windows or tabs.”/p>

  • Recommendation: Avoid breaking the back button by opening links to other web pages with HTML content in the SAME tab/window and only opening a NEW tab/window when linking to non-web content such as downloadable PDFs or other files.
